# MHRA Borderline Guidance: When a Supplement Becomes a Medicine

> Originally developed for a supplement subscription business, now genericized for universal use.

## The Core Rule

A product is classified by its **presentation** (what you say about it) and its **function** (what it does), not by what you call it. Calling something a "food supplement" does not make it one if the claims or composition suggest otherwise.

**If a product is classified as a medicine, it:**
- Cannot be sold as a food supplement
- Requires a **marketing authorisation** (extremely expensive and time-consuming)
- Must meet pharmaceutical manufacturing standards (GMP, not just food-grade)
- Is illegal to sell without MHRA authorisation

---

## The Three Tests for Medicinal Classification

The MHRA applies three criteria. If any one is met, the product is likely a medicine:

### Test 1: Medicinal Claims (Presentation)

Does the product claim to **treat, prevent, cure, diagnose, or alleviate** a disease, injury, or adverse condition?

**Common trigger words:**
- Treats, cures, heals, remedies
- Prevents, protects against, stops
- Relieves, alleviates, eases, soothes
- Reduces [symptom/disease]
- Restores, repairs, regenerates
- Anti-[disease] (anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, anti-ageing, anti-depressant)
- For [medical condition]
- Helps with [disease/symptom]

**Brand-specific examples:**

| Prohibited (Medicinal) | Permitted (Food) |
|----------------------|------------------|
| "Prevents osteoporosis" | "Vitamin D contributes to the maintenance of normal bones" |
| "Treats iron deficiency anaemia" | "Iron contributes to normal formation of red blood cells and haemoglobin" |
| "Reduces inflammation" | "Vitamin C contributes to normal collagen formation" |
| "Relieves joint pain" | "Vitamin C contributes to normal collagen formation for the normal function of cartilage" |
| "Lowers blood pressure" | "[Potassium] contributes to the maintenance of normal blood pressure" (if on Register) |
| "Boosts testosterone" | "Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels in the blood" |
| "Cures insomnia" | "Magnesium contributes to normal functioning of the nervous system" |
| "Prevents heart disease" | "Thiamine contributes to the normal function of the heart" |

### Test 2: Pharmacological Action (Function)

Does the product contain an ingredient that has a **pharmacological, immunological, or metabolic action**?

This is assessed by:
- **Dose:** Is the ingredient at a dose that has pharmacological effects?
- **Route of administration:** Is it oral, topical, injectable?
- **Composition:** Does it contain substances known to be pharmacologically active?
- **Context:** How is the product used by consumers?

**Examples of pharmacological action in supplements:**

| Ingredient + Dose | Risk Level | Why |
|-------------------|-----------|-----|
| Aspirin (any dose) | **Medicine** | Known pharmaceutical |
| Ibuprofen (any dose) | **Medicine** | Known pharmaceutical |
| DHEA (any dose) | **Medicine** | Controlled substance; hormone |
| Melatonin (UK) | **Medicine** | Prescription-only in UK |
| 5-HTP (isolated) | **High risk** | Precursor to serotonin; pharmacological |
| GABA (supplemental dose) | **High risk** | Neurotransmitter; pharmacological |
| L-DOPA (from Mucuna) | **Medicine** | Parkinson's drug precursor |
| High-dose nicotinic acid (>50mg) | **Risk** | Pharmacological dose for cholesterol |
| Very high-dose vitamin A (>3000µg) | **Risk** | Teratogenic; medicinal territory |
| Yohimbine | **Medicine/Illegal** | Prescription drug in many countries |
| Ephedra / Ephedrine | **Illegal** | Banned stimulant |
| Sildenafil (hidden in "male enhancement") | **Illegal medicine** | Criminal offence |

**Safe dose principle:** Keep all ingredients well below pharmacological thresholds. Design formulations as "nutritional support" not "therapeutic intervention."

### Test 3: Presentation as a Medicine

Is the product presented in a way that suggests it is a medicine?

**Medicinal presentation red flags:**
- Packaging that looks like pharmaceutical packaging (blister packs of pills, medicine-style bottles)
- Use of pharmaceutical terminology ("dosage", "treatment", "regimen", "therapy")
- References to clinical trials in a way that implies medicinal efficacy
- Comparisons to medicines ("natural alternative to [drug name]")
- Claims about mechanisms that are pharmacological ("inhibits COX-2", "blocks serotonin reuptake")
- Targeting people with specific diseases ("For diabetics", "For people with arthritis")
- Doctor/health professional endorsements implying medical use

## Borderline Case Studies

### Case 1: Glucosamine for Joint Health

**Scenario:** Product contains glucosamine sulfate and claims "Supports joint health."

**Analysis:**
- Glucosamine at food supplement doses is generally accepted as a food supplement
- "Supports joint health" is vague but acceptable if not linked to disease treatment
- However, if the claim was "Treats osteoarthritis" or "Repairs cartilage damage" → **medicinal**
- If the dose was at pharmaceutical levels (e.g., 1500mg+ with chondroitin) → **borderline**

**Verdict:** Acceptable as a food supplement with careful claim wording.

### Case 2: St John's Wort for Mood

**Scenario:** Product contains St John's Wort extract and claims "Supports emotional wellbeing."

**Analysis:**
- St John's Wort is a **traditional herbal medicine** (THR) in the UK, not a food supplement
- It has well-documented pharmacological interactions (SSRIs, contraceptives, warfarin)
- In many EU countries, St John's Wort products are classified as medicines
- "Emotional wellbeing" is vague but the ingredient itself is borderline

**Verdict:** **High risk.** In the UK, St John's Wort is typically sold under Traditional Herbal Registration (THR), not as a food supplement. Avoid for [Your Brand].

### Case 3: High-Dose Niacin for Cholesterol

**Scenario:** Product contains 500mg nicotinic acid and claims "Supports cardiovascular health."

**Analysis:**
- Niacin at 500mg causes flushing and is used pharmaceutically to modify lipid profiles
- The pharmacological dose for cholesterol is 1,000–2,000mg/day
- 500mg is below the pharmaceutical dose but well above nutritional needs (NRV = 16mg)
- EFSA UL for nicotinic acid = 10mg (as nicotinic acid)

**Verdict:** **High risk.** 500mg nicotinic acid is pharmacologically active (flushing, vasodilation). Would likely be classified as a medicine or at minimum borderline. Use nicotinamide form instead if B3 is needed for nutritional purposes.

### Case 4: Men's Vitality Product

**Scenario:** Product contains zinc, vitamin D, and fenugreek, marketed as "Men's Vitality" with imagery of muscular men.

**Analysis:**
- Zinc + vitamin D at nutritional doses = acceptable food supplement
- "Men's Vitality" is acceptable as a product name if claims stay within approved health claims
- However, if the website copy says "Boosts testosterone", "Builds muscle", "Increases libido" → **borderline/medicinal**
- Fenugreek is not a novel food but has no approved claims; if any claims are made about it → unauthorised

**Verdict:** Acceptable ONLY if:
- Claims are limited to: "Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels in the blood"
- No implied medicinal or performance-enhancing claims
- No imagery suggesting medical/pharmaceutical outcomes

### Case 5: "Detox" Tea

**Scenario:** Product contains various herbs and claims to "detox your liver."

**Analysis:**
- "Detox" is not a recognised physiological process for foods
- "Detox your liver" implies the liver is toxic and needs cleansing → **medicinal-adjacent**
- Liver-targeting claims are high-risk
- Many "detox" products have been removed from sale by Trading Standards

**Verdict:** **Not acceptable.** Remove all "detox" language. Use only approved claims (e.g., "Choline contributes to normal lipid metabolism" if applicable).

### Case 6: CBD Oil Supplement

**Scenario:** Product contains CBD and claims "Promotes relaxation."

**Analysis:**
- CBD is a **novel food** in the EU/UK and requires authorisation
- CBD products marketed with health claims have been subject to MHRA borderline assessment
- "Promotes relaxation" is not on the Register
- The FSA has a specific CBD authorisation process

**Verdict:** **Not acceptable for [Your Brand].** CBD requires specific novel food authorisation and is high-risk for borderline issues. Do not use.

## MHRA Borderline Advice Service

If you are uncertain about a product, you can request **informal borderline advice** from the MHRA:

**MHRA Borderline Unit**
- Email: borderline@mhra.gov.uk
- Include: Full product description, ingredients, doses, proposed claims, packaging mock-ups
- Response time: Several weeks to months
- Cost: Free for informal advice

**Note:** Informal advice is not legally binding but indicates the MHRA's likely position. For legal certainty, you need a formal classification, which is a more involved process.

## What Happens If MHRA Classifies Your Product as a Medicine

| Stage | Action |
|-------|--------|
| **Investigation** | MHRA reviews product after complaint or proactive monitoring |
| **Borderline determination** | MHRA issues opinion that the product is a medicine |
| **Notice to company** | You receive a letter requiring cessation of sale as a food |
| **Product recall** | All stock must be recalled from retailers/customers |
| **Potential prosecution** | Trading Standards may prosecute for selling an unlicensed medicine |
| **Penalties** | Unlimited fines, up to 2 years imprisonment (Crown Court) |
| **Reputational damage** | Public notification, brand harm |

**Prevention is vastly cheaper than cure.** Get borderline advice BEFORE production.

## Decision Framework for Borderline Assessment

```
New product or formulation
        │
        ▼
Does it contain any prescription/controlled substance?
   ├─ Yes → STOP. This is a medicine. Do not produce.
   └─ No  → Continue
              │
              ▼
Are any claims made about treating/preventing/curing disease?
   ├─ Yes → STOP. Redesign claims. This is medicinal presentation.
   └─ No  → Continue
              │
              ▼
Is any ingredient at a pharmacological dose?
   ├─ Yes → STOP. Lower dose or remove ingredient.
   └─ No  → Continue
              │
              ▼
Does the presentation suggest medicinal use?
   ├─ Yes → STOP. Redesign packaging, naming, imagery.
   └─ No  → Continue
              │
              ▼
Are there any borderline ingredients (herbs with THR, amino acids, hormones)?
   ├─ Yes → Seek MHRA informal advice before production.
   └─ No  → LOW RISK. Proceed with standard compliance checks.
```

## Summary: Keep [Your Brand] on the Food Side

| DO (Food Supplement) | DON'T (Medicine) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| Use exact EU Register health claims | Make therapeutic or disease claims |
| Keep doses nutritional, not pharmacological | Use pharmaceutical doses |
| Describe nutrition and normal body function | Describe treatment or correction of disease |
| Use lifestyle/wellness imagery | Use medical/pharmaceutical imagery |
| Name products descriptively ("Daily Wellness", "Gut Health") | Name products medicinally ("Testosterone Booster", "Anti-Inflammatory") |
| Target general wellness consumers | Target people with specific diseases |
| Focus on "contributes to normal function" | Focus on "improves", "enhances", "boosts" |
